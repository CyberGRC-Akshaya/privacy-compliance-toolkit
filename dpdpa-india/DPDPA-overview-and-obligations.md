# Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 — Complete Guide

> India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA) was passed on **11 August 2023**. This guide provides a comprehensive overview of obligations for Data Fiduciaries operating in India.

## 1. Overview & Background

The **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023** (DPDPA) is India's first comprehensive data protection legislation, replacing the IT Act's Section 43A and the IT (Reasonable Security Practices) Rules 2011 for personal data matters.

**Key milestones:**
- Justice Sri Krishna Committee Report: 2018 (initial framework)
- Personal Data Protection Bill 2019: Introduced, withdrawn 2022
- DPDPA 2023: Passed Lok Sabha (7 Aug 2023) and Rajya Sabha (9 Aug 2023)
- Presidential assent: 11 August 2023
- **Rules: Pending final notification (as of 2024)**
- Enforcement date: To be notified by Central Government

---

## 2. Territorial Scope

The DPDPA applies to:
- Processing of **digital personal data** within India
- Processing of digital personal data **outside India** where it relates to the offering of goods or services to Data Principals in India

**What is "personal data"?** Any data about an identifiable individual. The DPDPA covers **digital** personal data (collected/digitised).

## 3. Key Definitions

| Term | DPDPA Definition | GDPR Equivalent |
|------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Data Principal** | The individual whose personal data is processed | Data Subject |
| **Data Fiduciary** | Entity that determines purpose and means of processing | Data Controller |
| **Data Processor** | Entity that processes data on behalf of a Fiduciary | Data Processor |
| **Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF)** | Fiduciary notified by Central Government based on risk | No direct equivalent |
| **Consent Manager** | Entity that manages consent on behalf of Data Principals | — |
| **Personal Data** | Any data about an identifiable individual | Personal Data |

## 4. Legal Bases for Processing

Unlike GDPR's six legal bases, DPDPA primarily recognises two:

### 4.1 Consent (Section 6)
- Must be **free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous**
- Must be **explicit** (opt-in; pre-ticked boxes not valid)
- **Bundled consent** (processing for multiple purposes bundled together) is **not valid**
- Must be **revocable** at any time (as easy to withdraw as to give)
- **Children's data (under 18)**: Consent of parent/guardian required
- **Consent notice** must specify: data being collected, purpose, how to exercise rights, how to withdraw

### 4.2 Legitimate Uses (Section 7) — No Consent Required
- State and its instrumentalities for provision of services, subsidies, benefits
- Medical emergency threatening life
- Employment purposes (reasonable expectation of processing)
- Public interest purposes notified by Central Government

## 5. Data Principal Rights (Section 11–14)

| Right | Description | Fiduciary's Obligation |
|-------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Right to Information** (§11) | Know what data is processed | Provide clear notice |
| **Right to Correction & Erasure** (§12) | Correct inaccurate data; erase when no longer necessary | Process within [timeframe per Rules] |
| **Right to Grievance Redressal** (§13) | Raise concerns about processing | Grievance mechanism mandatory |
| **Right to Nominate** (§14) | Nominate another person to exercise rights in case of death/incapacity | Accept nominations |

---

## 6. Data Fiduciary Obligations

### 6.1 Notice Requirement (Section 5)
Before seeking consent, provide notice in:
- English or any official Indian language
- Containing: personal data being collected, purpose of processing, how to exercise rights, how to raise grievances, how to withdraw consent, how to make complaints to DPB

### 6.2 Purpose Limitation (Section 8)
- Process only for **specified lawful purpose**
- No further processing incompatible with original purpose
- **Retain only as long as necessary** for the purpose
- **Delete data** when purpose is accomplished and no legal obligation requires retention

### 6.3 Data Accuracy (Section 8)
- Make **reasonable efforts** to ensure accuracy of personal data
- Especially important where processing may have significant impact on Data Principal

### 6.4 Security Safeguards (Section 8)
- Implement **reasonable security safeguards** to prevent breach
- Notify **Data Protection Board (DPB) and affected individuals** in case of breach in manner prescribed by Rules

### 6.5 Children's Data (Section 9)
- Obtain **verifiable parental consent** before processing
- Do not process data **harmful to children**
- No **tracking, behavioural monitoring, or targeted advertising** directed at children

---

## 7. Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDF) — Section 10

Central Government may notify certain Data Fiduciaries as "Significant" based on:
- Volume and sensitivity of data processed
- Risk to rights of Data Principals
- Potential impact on sovereignty and integrity
- Risk to electoral democracy
- National security implications

**Additional SDF Obligations:**
- Appoint a **Data Protection Officer (DPO)** based in India
- Appoint an **independent Data Auditor**
- Conduct **Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIA)**
- Conduct **periodic audits**

---

## 8. Cross-Border Data Transfers (Section 16)

- Personal data **may be transferred** to countries notified by Central Government
- Countries NOT on the approved list: Transfer **restricted**
- Rules will specify approved countries (whitelist approach vs. GDPR's adequacy decisions)

**Practical implication:** Monitor the approved country list notification; review all international data flows for DPDPA compliance.

---

## 9. Penalties (Section 33)

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Failure to protect data (breach due to inadequate safeguards) | ₹250 crore (~$30 million) |
| Failure to notify breach to DPB/individuals | ₹200 crore (~$24 million) |
| Children's data violations | ₹200 crore |
| Failure to comply with DPB directions | ₹150 crore |
| Other violations | ₹50 crore per violation |

---

## 10. Data Protection Board (DPB) — Section 18

- Quasi-judicial body established by Central Government
- Powers: Investigate, adjudicate, impose penalties
- **Data Principals can file complaints** with DPB after exhausting Fiduciary's grievance mechanism
- DPB can suo motu investigate breaches

---

## 11. GDPR vs DPDPA Comparison

| Dimension | GDPR | DPDPA 2023 |
|-----------|------|------------|
| Legal bases for processing | 6 bases | Consent + Legitimate Uses |
| Right to erasure | ✅ Explicit (Art. 17) | ✅ Section 12 |
| Right to portability | ✅ Art. 20 | ❌ Not included |
| Right to object | ✅ Art. 21 | Limited (withdrawal of consent) |
| DPO requirement | For controllers meeting criteria | For Significant Data Fiduciaries |
| DPIA requirement | High-risk processing | For Significant Data Fiduciaries |
| Breach notification | 72 hours to supervisory authority | To DPB (timeframe per Rules) |
| Cross-border transfers | Adequacy / safeguards / derogations | Whitelist of permitted countries |
| Extraterritorial scope | ✅ Strong | ✅ Applies to India-directed processing |
| Max penalty | €20 million / 4% global revenue | ₹250 crore (~$30M) |

## 12. Compliance Roadmap for Data Fiduciaries

### Immediate (Before Enforcement)
- [ ] Data mapping exercise — identify all personal data collected, purpose, retention
- [ ] Review consent mechanisms — ensure DPDPA-compliant (opt-in, specific, revocable)
- [ ] Update privacy notices for DPDPA requirements
- [ ] Establish grievance mechanism
- [ ] Assess cross-border data transfers

### Short-Term
- [ ] Data retention policy review — delete data with no valid purpose
- [ ] Children's data: identify and implement parental consent
- [ ] Contracts with Data Processors: update with DPDPA requirements
- [ ] Internal training: awareness of DPDPA obligations

### If Notified as Significant Data Fiduciary
- [ ] Appoint India-resident DPO
- [ ] Engage independent Data Auditor
- [ ] Implement DPIA process
- [ ] Periodic audit programme
